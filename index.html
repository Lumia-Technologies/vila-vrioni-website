The Estate

A Manor in the City of a Thousand Windows

In the heart of Berat — Albania's UNESCO World Heritage city and one of Europe's last undiscovered corners — stands the ancestral compound of the Vrioni family, one of the most distinguished aristocratic dynasties in Albanian history.

Officially designated as a protected cultural monument under the name Objekti i Yzedin Galip Pashë Vrioni, the compound represents Albania's finest surviving example of late Ottoman residential architecture. Its two-storey rendered main building, vaulted stone outbuildings, stone-paved courtyard, and intricately coffered wooden ceilings speak to a world of wealth, civic pride, and refined craftsmanship that few properties in the region can match.

Confiscated during the communist dictatorship and left to decay for decades, the villa has been the subject of a meticulous, museum-quality restoration since 2021. Today, a descendant of Izedin Vrioni is returning the compound to its original splendour — stone by stone, timber by timber.

Heritage & Legacy

The Vrioni Family

From Ottoman pashas to Albanian nation-builders — the Vrioni story spans four centuries of Albanian history, from a village near Berat to the halls of power, and back to ruin and redemption.

The Vrioni (Ottoman Turkish: Viryon-zâdeler) were among Albania's largest landowning families, their origins traceable to the village of Virjon near Berat. By the mid-17th century they appear as timarli sipahi in the Janina Vilayet; a 1685 fountain inscription bears the family genealogy. Over generations the family branched into the Berat line (bearing the title bey) and the Fier line (bearing the title pasha). The sanjak and city of Berat was dominated by the Vrioni family throughout the 19th century.

Their influence bridged Ottoman administration, Albanian patriotism, the Declaration of Independence of 1912, and the formation of the modern Albanian state — until the communist takeover of 1944 targeted them as class enemies, confiscated their estates, demolished their palaces, and persecuted family members with extraordinary brutality.

Izedin Vrioni — Mayor of Berat

Izedin Vrioni (also written Yzedin Bej Vrioni) was the son of Galip Pasha Vrioni of the Fier branch and Nasip hanëm Vlora, and brother of Qemal Bey Vrioni. He is the family member most closely associated with this property — an official 2016 Albanian heritage decision names the protected building Objekti i Yzedin Galip Pashë Vrioni.

He served as one of Berat's most prominent mayors across the formative decades of Albanian independence, repeatedly called upon for his leadership in a politically volatile era. He also commanded the Berat forces during the Vlora War of June–August 1920 — the decisive confrontation between Albanian nationalists and the Kingdom of Italy, whose Albanian victory compelled Italy to relinquish Vlorë and formally recognise Albania's 1913 borders.

Son of Prime Minister Iliaz; the renowned French translator of Ismail Kadare. Arrested 1947, sentenced to 15 years hard labour. After the fall of communism: appointed Albania's Ambassador to UNESCO.

Under Communist Rule, 1944–1990

After the communist takeover in 1944 the Vrioni family was branded "class enemies" — feudal bloodsuckers, enemies of the people. Their estates were nationalised under a series of confiscation laws; their historic Berat sarays were demolished in 1947. For forty years the Vrioni name was used as the archetypal symbol of oppression in Albanian school textbooks — a deliberate, systematic erasure of their historical contributions.

  • Izedin Bej Vrioni — beaten to death in the local police station, 1947. His remains have never been found.
  • Sami Bej Vrioni — died under torture, 1947.
  • Qemal Bej Vrioni — sentenced to life imprisonment by the Special Court (1945); died in Burrel prison, c. 1950–1952.
  • Jusuf Vrioni — sentenced to 15 years hard labour; later emerged as Albania's greatest literary translator.
  • Irfan Bej Vrioni — sentenced to life imprisonment.
  • Kahreman Pasha Vrioni — died under unclear circumstances, 1955.
  • Nermin Vrioni — died homeless, suffering from dementia.
  • Iliaz Vrioni's remains — exhumed from Paris in 1946 and thrown into the Osum River in Berat. He has no grave.

In 2009 the European Court of Human Rights ruled against Albania in Vrioni and Others v. Albania and Italy (App. nos. 35720/04 & 42832/06), finding violations of the right to property and fair trial, with compensation of approximately €1.9 million awarded to the heirs.

Nevin Vrioni

Nevin Vrioni was born in this house. She lived within these walls until she was thirteen years old, when the communist regime confiscated everything — the villa, the estates, the name itself. Today, her grandchild is restoring Vila Vrioni — not just as a building, but as a promise kept across generations.

The Journey

Restoration of Vila Vrioni

A meticulous, ongoing effort to return this heritage building to its former dignity — from the first emergency interventions in 2018 to the present day.

2012
Survey & Assessment

A detailed architectural survey commissioned by the Institute of Cultural Monuments documents every facade, floor plan, and decorative element of the compound — from the Ottoman-era hamami with its domed ceiling to the bas-relief carvings of the dhoma e mire (finest room). The survey reveals the full scale of damage after decades of neglect and misuse under the communist regime, and forms the basis for the restoration masterplan.

2018
Roof Reconstruction — Emergency Stabilisation

Restoration begins with the most urgent priority: securing the building itself. The original roof structure, weakened by decades of neglect under and after the communist regime, is fully reconstructed to halt further water damage and structural deterioration. With the roof sealed and the building protected from the elements, the foundation is laid for the comprehensive restoration to follow — work that has continued without interruption to the present day.

2021
July — Early Phase

Restoration begins in earnest. Scaffolding envelops the main facade as structural assessment is completed and the heritage designation placard is mounted. Inside, decades of communist-era paint are painstakingly stripped from the walls — each layer a stratum of different eras, the geological record of a building put to many involuntary uses. The original lime plaster begins to re-emerge.

2022
July — Structural Phase

Major structural work advances. The basement vaults — their stone arches intact after more than a century — are excavated and consolidated. Floors are stripped back to their original timber structure. Throughout the building, construction materials accumulate as the scale of the task becomes clear. A remarkable discovery: original wooden ceilings, including a magnificent oval-medallion panel, survive beneath communist-era coverings.

2023
August — Craftsmanship Phase

The stone outbuilding — a traditional Albanian konak — takes shape with extraordinary clarity. Local craftsmen lay dressed limestone paving in the courtyard and fit new arched windows of dark-stained timber, faithful to the originals. In the main building, the restoration of the wooden coffered ceilings reaches a spectacular milestone: room-by-room, intricate hand-crafted panels of richly oiled walnut recreate the original interiors to a standard that would not shame a palace.

2025
April — Continuing Progress

Work continues across both the main villa and the outbuildings as finishing work advances room by room. Video documentation captures the current state of the restoration. The vision of a fully completed manor — serving as both a memorial to the Vrioni family's legacy and a living cultural heritage site within UNESCO-listed Berat — draws steadily closer.
