Berat

City of a Thousand Windows  ·  UNESCO World Heritage since 2008
World Heritage

A Living Museum of Civilisations

One of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in Europe, Berat is a place where 2,400 years of history remain visible — not in ruins, but in the fabric of daily life.

Berat was inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 2008 as part of the "Historic Centres of Berat and Gjirokastra," recognised as rare examples of well-preserved Ottoman towns that illustrate the coexistence of diverse religious and cultural communities across centuries. The inscription cites Berat's exceptional testimony to the architectural character of the Ottoman period, while acknowledging the far deeper strata — Illyrian, Roman, Byzantine — that underlie the Ottoman surface.

The city's celebrated epithet, the City of a Thousand Windows, refers to the white Ottoman houses that cascade down the hillside of the Mangalem quarter, their rows of tall wooden windows reflecting the Adriatic light and creating a dazzling, almost theatrical effect against the dark rock of Mount Tomorr behind. It is a skyline unlike any other in Europe — one that has survived war, ideology, and time itself.

Ancient Origins

Antipatrea to Pulcheriopolis

Ceramic finds from the seventh century BC attest to the earliest settlement on the rocky hill that would become Berat — the stronghold of the Illyrian Desaretes tribe, who raised the first fortifications above the Osum River.

By 314 BC the settlement had been named Antipatrea, in honour of Antipater, regent of Macedon and successor to Alexander the Great. Its strategic position — commanding a valley passage between the coastal plain and the mountainous interior — made it a prize fought over for centuries. The Romans arrived in 200 BC and besieged the city with devastating force, incorporating it into the province of Macedonia. Roman occupation brought new engineering techniques: roads, aqueducts, and water systems, parts of which survive in archaeological trace to the present day.

Under Byzantine rule the city was renamed Pulcheriopolis, in honour of Pulcheria, sister of Emperor Theodosius II (r. 408–450). The Byzantines transformed Berat into a fortified religious and administrative centre, reinforcing the citadel walls in the fifth century and again under Emperor Justinian I in the sixth. During the thirteenth century, under Michael I Komnenos Doukas, Despot of Epirus, the Kala (citadel) was rebuilt into the imposing fortress that still crowns the city — its walls enclosing some twenty churches, many adorned with frescoes by master painters of the period.

Ottoman Era

Four Centuries of the Crescent and the Cross

The Ottoman conquest of 1417 opened a transformative chapter in Berat's history — one that, remarkably, did not obliterate the past but layered itself upon it.

Where other conquests razed what came before, the Ottomans in Berat built alongside the existing fabric. The castle's Byzantine churches continued in use even as mosques rose within the same walls. The result is a cityscape of extraordinary religious coexistence: minarets beside bell towers, the muezzin's call within earshot of Orthodox liturgy. It is precisely this layering — this refusal of one tradition to erase another — that UNESCO would cite five centuries later as Berat's outstanding universal value.

Beneath the Kala, the city grew into two distinct quarters on opposite banks of the Osum. Mangalem, on the eastern bank, became the predominantly Muslim quarter — its steep hillside terraced with the distinctive whitewashed houses, each with rows of tall windows arranged so closely as to give the impression of a wall of glass. It is the Mangalem skyline, more than any other, that earned Berat its epithet. Gorica, across the river, developed as the Christian quarter, connected to Mangalem by the famous stone Gorica Bridge — a structure that became, both literally and symbolically, a span between faiths and communities.

Within the Kala, the Church of the Dormition of the Theotokos (the Cathedral of Saint Mary) houses the Onufri Museum, named for the sixteenth-century master Onufri, one of Albania's most celebrated icon painters. The museum's collection of over two hundred icons and liturgical objects — spanning the fourteenth to the twentieth century — represents one of the finest assemblages of post-Byzantine art in southeastern Europe.

It was during the Ottoman centuries that the great families of Berat rose to prominence: the Vloras, the Toptanis, and above all the Vrioni — who would come to dominate the sanjak of Berat and shape the city's identity for generations.

Dynasty

The Vrioni Legacy in Berat

No family is more deeply woven into the history of Berat than the Vrioni — from the village of Virjon near Berat, they rose across two centuries to become Ottoman generals, Albanian prime ministers, and signatories of the Declaration of Independence.

The Vrioni (Viryon-zâdeler in Ottoman Turkish) appear in the historical record from the mid-seventeenth century as timarli sipahi — timariot cavalry integrated into the Ottoman military-administrative system of the Janina Vilayet. A fountain inscription dated 1685 in the village of Virjon (today Ullinjas) records the family genealogy, making it one of the earliest documented Albanian noble lineages. By the nineteenth century, the Vrioni exerted considerable dominance over the sanjak and city of Berat.

Their influence extended to the halls of European diplomacy: Mehmet Ali Pasha Vrioni served as Vice-President of the League of Prizren in 1878 and traveled to Paris, Berlin, and Vienna to press Albanian national demands. His son Iliaz Bey Vrioni was three times Prime Minister of Albania and a signatory of the Declaration of Independence in 1912. Nyzhet Bey Vrioni and Kahreman Pasha Vrioni funded the first Albanian-language school in Berat and supported the Elbasan Normal School — cornerstones of the Albanian national awakening.

And it was Izedin Vrioni, Mayor of Berat across the formative decades of Albanian statehood and commander of the Berat forces during the Vlora War of 1920, who built and occupied the manor that bears his name to this day — the protected cultural monument now being restored as Vila Vrioni.

Modern History

Independence, Revolution, and Rebirth

Berat has stood at the centre of Albanian history at each of its decisive turns — from the national awakening to the communist revolution, and now the country's emergence as one of Europe's most compelling cultural destinations.

The Albanian national awakening (Rilindja) of the late nineteenth century found fertile ground in Berat. The city's educated élite — the Vrioni, Vlora, and Toptani families among them — were active in the movement for Albanian-language education and political autonomy. Several Berat delegates attended the Assembly of Vlora in 1912, where Albania declared its independence from the Ottoman Empire. The Vrioni contributed two signatories: Iliaz Bey and Sami Bey Vrioni.

In October 1944, the Congress of Berat marked a darker turning point. Convened by the National Liberation Front, the congress established a provisional communist government under Enver Hoxha — a government that, within months, would install itself in Tirana and inaugurate nearly half a century of totalitarian rule. The Vrioni and other aristocratic families of Berat were among the first to be targeted: their estates confiscated, their palaces demolished, their members imprisoned, tortured, or killed.

After the fall of communism in 1991, Berat began a slow rediscovery of its own heritage. The UNESCO inscription of 2008 recognised what the city's residents had always known: that Berat is not a relic but a living city, one whose layered history — Illyrian, Roman, Byzantine, Ottoman, Albanian — is written not in museum catalogues but in the stones, windows, and bridges of everyday life. Today, as Albania opens to the world, Berat stands as one of the most authentic and unspoiled heritage destinations in the Mediterranean.
