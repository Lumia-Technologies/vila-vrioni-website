The Market

Albania's Tourism Revolution

A country that barely registered on the global tourism map a decade ago is now one of Europe's fastest-growing destinations — and the trajectory is far from over.

Albania recorded 11.7 million foreign visitors in 2024, an 82% increase from the 6.4 million who arrived in 2019. Momentum continued into 2025, with arrivals reaching a historic peak of 12.5 million — a further 6.6% increase. International tourists spent approximately €5 billion in Albania in 2024 alone, a 20% increase year-on-year.

The Albanian government has set an ambitious target of 30 million annual visitors by 2030. Major international hotel brands are entering the market — 11 global brands have already invested, with 11 more in active negotiations. EU candidate status, granted in 2014 with formal accession negotiations opening in 2022, continues to drive infrastructure development and investor confidence.

The Destination

Why Berat

Where Ottoman architecture, UNESCO heritage, and one of Europe's oldest continuously inhabited cities converge — with a fraction of the crowds and costs of comparable Mediterranean destinations.

Berat is known as the City of a Thousand Windows — a name earned by its extraordinary hillside of white Ottoman houses, their windows stacked in tiers above the Osum River. Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2008, Berat bears witness to the coexistence of diverse religious and cultural communities across centuries: Byzantine churches, Ottoman mosques, and one of the world's last inhabited castle-towns share the same ancient stones.

The city sits at the heart of Albania's cultural interior, two hours from Tirana International Airport and connected by improving infrastructure. Yet unlike Dubrovnik, Kotor, or Santorini — destinations that have been loved to the point of erosion — Berat remains deeply authentic. Tourism accommodation stock in the municipality grew 23% between 2019 and 2023, with 68% of new developments involving adaptive reuse of historic fabric. Boutique hotel conversions drove most of this activity.

The Opportunity

A Window Before It Closes

Albania today is where Croatia was fifteen years ago, and where Montenegro was ten years ago — except with stronger growth, lower entry costs, and an EU accession timeline that is compressing opportunity into years, not decades.

Property prices in Albania start from €900–1,200/m² in secondary cities and reach €1,600–3,000/m² in prime locations — compared to €3,500–4,500/m² in Croatia and €2,500–4,000/m² in Greece. Short-term rental yields in Albania reach 8–10% annually, among the highest in Europe. Housing prices rose 8% nationally in 2024, the strongest growth in five years.

Berat itself is seeing nearly four times the tourist volume it had in 2019. The World Bank's Albania Cultural Heritage Site Management programme began physical works in Berat in 2026, injecting €8.5 million into conservation construction — a 340% increase over public heritage investment two years earlier. This kind of multilateral commitment signals long-term institutional confidence in the city's heritage value.

The Anchor Project

Vila Vrioni

A protected late Ottoman manor in Berat's historic Mangalem quarter — approved for restoration by Albania's Ministry of Culture, with a masterplan for 13 exclusive guest rooms and full heritage hospitality infrastructure.

Vila Vrioni is not a speculative build. It is a Category 1 Cultural Monument with four centuries of documented provenance, an approved restoration project by Prof. Dr. Pirro Thomo and the Institute of Cultural Monuments, and a European Court of Human Rights ruling affirming the family's rightful ownership. The property is being restored by a direct descendant of Izedin Vrioni, Mayor of Berat — the man whose name the building officially bears.

The masterplan, designed in accordance with Albania's monument protection regulations, transforms the compound into an intimate luxury hotel with a distinctive cultural programme: an ethnographic museum, a restored Ottoman hamam, a historic tea tower, courtyard dining, and thirteen guest rooms across two restored heritage buildings — all within 1,364 square metres of walled compound in the UNESCO buffer zone.
